Cotton Fleahoppers

Source: TAMU Entomology extension

Cotton fleahoppers (Pseudatomoscelis seriatus) are small (1/8 inch long), pale green hemipteran pests of cotton. They suck sap from the terminal, tender parts of the plant such as young leaves and small squares (flower buds), and cause the squares to die and fall off. Hence, it is considered a pest of cotton during the early squaring stages, but not of late-stage cotton. Cotton is especially susceptible to damage from fleahoppers during the first 3 weeks of squaring.

Source: Aslam et al., 2020

Cotton fleahoppers overwinter as eggs primarily in woolly croton; however they may also overwinter in other hosts such as horsemint and cutleaf evening primrose. Diapause exit is triggered in the Spring in response to elevation in temperatures and rainfall. Fleahopper numbers build up on these alternate hosts. When these become unfavorable for further feeding (plant matures, or due to weed control), fleahoppers seek out other hosts, including cotton, to colonize and feed from.​
